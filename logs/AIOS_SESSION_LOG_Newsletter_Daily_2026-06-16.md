## 4. VOICE DNA OUTPUT (Stage 1 Final)

### <voice_dna>

**1. SENTENCE ARCHITECTURE**
Long, multi-clause sentences built on commas, not short punchy ones. Let a thought run through 3-4 sub-clauses before stopping. Stack rhetorical questions in runs of 3-5 with no answer in between, letting them accumulate pressure before addressing any. Paragraphs open on transitional words doing structural work: "Eventually," "However," "Now," "So," "Through analysis and reflection." Use semicolons occasionally for a hard pivot; em dashes rarely but specifically — always to insert a clarifying aside mid-sentence, never for dramatic pause.

**2. VOCABULARY FINGERPRINT**
Over-indexed: affirmations, habits, beliefs, identity, reality, eventually, however, therefore, consistently. Reach for concrete autobiographical numbers as proof, not vague time references — "eight years old," "thirty-minute sessions," "12th December 2023," "under two years." Technical-vs-plain ratio is almost entirely plain; explain through scene and analogy (the pool table) rather than terminology.

**3. EMOTIONAL REGISTER**
Confessional but not dramatized — state hard facts (alcohol, mother leaving, father's suicide, shack with no toilet) in the same flat declarative tone as everything else, then move immediately to the lesson. No lingering, no self-pity, no need to make the reader feel sorry. Write directly at the reader using "you," shifting fluidly between memoir and instruction within the same section.

**4. STRUCTURAL SIGNATURES**
Open sections on a broad question or a flat statement of fact, not a cold-open scene. Close on a reflective question, an imperative, or occasionally a borrowed quote (Nietzsche). Analogy is the primary teaching tool, committed fully — the pool metaphor runs for three full pages, not one paragraph.

**5. CONSTRAINT VOICE**
Trinidad and financial hardship show up as plain remembered fact, never as a sympathy bid. "We lived in a shack on the side of a highway without a toilet" sits in the same sentence-weight as "I went from a man living paycheck to paycheck to a man with a wife, one child, a house." The constraint is reported, not performed.

### <negative_vocabulary>

Never write: motivational-poster phrasing without a mechanism behind it; sarcasm; cynicism; em dash for dramatic effect (only for clarifying asides, rarely); short fragment sentences for punch; corporate jargon (synergy, circle back, touch base); emoji.

**FLAG — contradicts existing Production OS rules:**
User wrote "I've utilized affirmations" and used "transform/transforming" three times in book excerpt. Newsletter DO NOT list (Section 5.1 of Prompt Library) currently bans both "utilize" and "transformative." Decision pending: keep ban as craft choice for newsletter register, or relax per Stage 1 findings.

### <voice_wrapper>

Write in long, comma-built sentences that let one thought run through several clauses before resolving. Stack rhetorical questions in runs of three to five before answering any of them. Open sections on a flat factual statement or broad question, never a dramatized cold-open. State hard personal facts plainly, in the same tone as everything else — no lingering, no sympathy bid. Move from memoir into direct instruction using "you" without a hard transition. Anchor every claim in a concrete number from real life. Commit fully to one analogy rather than switching between several. Close on a reflective question or a direct imperative.
